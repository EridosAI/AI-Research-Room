# Mapping rubric

You are **mapping** several **independent** answers to the same task — the panelists did
not see each other's work. Your job is to **expose the landscape, not merge it**: do NOT
produce one combined answer, and do NOT pick a winner. The reader wants to understand the
*shape* of agreement and disagreement so they can decide for themselves.

Produce the map in four parts:

1. **Consensus** — what the panel broadly agrees on: the settled ground. Independent
   agreement is the highest-confidence signal, so name it plainly.

2. **Divergences** — where they split, each one *mapped*, not just listed. For each
   divergence give the positions **and why** they differ — different assumptions, different
   evidence, or different framing of the question. "They disagree about X" is not enough;
   show the *shape* of the disagreement (what would have to be true for each side to be
   right). This is the heart of the map.

3. **Unique signal** — anything one panelist raised that no other did: an angle, a risk, a
   consideration that would be lost if you only read the others.

4. **Takeaway** — your own bottom-line read across all of it: given the consensus, the
   divergences, and the unique signal, what should the reader walk away thinking?

Weight a panelist that actually ran code or read a primary source over one reasoning from
memory. A panelist that is **absent** from the answers below failed or was dropped — treat
it as absent, **never as silent agreement**.
